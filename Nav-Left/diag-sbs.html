Step-by-step diagnostic approach

The diagnosis of sudden cardiac arrest is a definitive one. The patient is unresponsive, and assessment of airway, breathing, and circulation shows absence of normal breathing and no signs of circulation. Assessment of the specific cardiac rhythm disturbance responsible should be immediate, whether by automated external defibrillator or other cardiac monitoring, and is essential for management. Possibilities include ventricular fibrillation, pulseless ventricular tachycardia (VT), View image pulseless electrical activity (PEA), and asystole. Torsades de pointes is a sub-group of polymorphic VT in patients with an underlying prolonged QT interval. View image

Clinical assessment

A thorough search must be made for the underlying cause of cardiac arrest both during resuscitation and following successful resuscitation. The physical examination may demonstrate an elevated jugular venous pulse with decompensated heart failure. A history of trauma should prompt suspicion of cardiac tamponade, valvular disturbance, myocardial puncture, or tension pneumothorax. Tracheal deviation suggests tension pneumothorax. A murmur may indicate a valvular cause of compromise, which might lead to sudden cardiac arrest. Pulmonary auscultation may reveal bilateral crepitations in decompensated heart failure, absent air entry in pneumothorax, or signs of underlying pulmonary disease. Neurological examination may reveal focal signs suggestive of intracranial pathology. Finally, the need for endotracheal intubation should be assessed and addressed.

The patient’s medical family and medicine history should be reviewed at the earliest opportunity to identify possible causes. Clues that may be identified include:

  • Recent symptoms: chest pain may indicate myocardial ischaemia. Syncope may indicate structural heart disease or pre-existing arrhythmias. Palpitations may indicate pre-existing arrhythmias.

  • Past medical history: potential causes include CAD and its associated risk factors (e.g., hypertension, diabetes mellitus, hypercholesterolaemia), left ventricular dysfunction, hypertrophic cardiomyopathy, long QT syndrome, structural heart disease, use of illicit substances, medicines (including those that cause QT prolongation or diuretics that cause electrolyte disturbances), kidney disease, and history of eating disorders (which may contribute to hypokalaemia and/or hypophosphataemia). [37]

  • Family history of sudden cardiac arrest: a history of sudden cardiac arrest may be due to familial QT-interval prolongation syndromes or familial cardiomyopathies. A family history of CAD may also be present.

Investigations

Several tests are required immediately to assess the underlying cause of the arrest and the condition of the patient:

  • Patients require continuous cardiac monitoring to assess the rhythm.

  • An ECG should also be performed immediately and then subsequently to assess for evolving changes. Signs revealed by the ECG may include prolonged QT interval, ST-segment or T-wave changes (indicating ischaemia), conduction abnormalities, ventricular hypertrophy, QRS prolongation in V1 through V3 and/or epsilon waves in cardiomyopathy, and T-wave inversion in V1 through V3 in arrhythmogenic right ventricular dysplasia (ARVD).

  • A FBC should be performed to look for haemorrhage.

  • Serum electrolytes should be performed to search for electrolyte abnormalities; these may occur as a consequence of cardiac arrest, as well as being causes.

  • ABG measurement is essential to assess acid-base status and may reveal respiratory acidosis, metabolic acidosis, respiratory acidosis with renal compensation, metabolic acidosis with respiratory compensation, or mixed metabolic and respiratory acidosis. Respiratory and metabolic parameters should be optimised as necessary to normalise acid-base status. The abnormal results may be a result of sudden cardiac arrest itself rather than the underlying cause.

  • Cardiac biomarkers should be measured. However, elevations in markers of MI may be a result of sudden cardiac arrest and do not necessarily mean that the underlying cause was an MI.

  • A toxicology screen is essential to rule out illicit substances that may predispose to ventricular arrhythmia.

  • Chest x-ray may reveal a pneumothorax, pulmonary oedema, or other disorders of the lungs. The thoracic cage may show causes of, or complications from, cardiac arrest. Endotracheal tube placement should be evaluated if the patient is intubated.

  • Echocardiogram can be used to assess cardiac activity acutely during resuscitation. Acute complications should be assessed and tamponade and valve disorders excluded, especially in PEA. Left ventricular function should be assessed 48 hours later, after the period of post-arrest myocardial stunning. [38]

  • Coronary angiography: coronary disease is a predisposing factor for sudden cardiac arrest. Assessment should be made immediately, if possible, in the setting of ST-segment elevation MI, recurrent ventricular arrhythmia, or cardiogenic shock, [39] and as soon as possible in the setting of non-ST-segment elevation MI or sudden cardiac arrest of unknown aetiology. [15] [40]

Other tests to consider include:

  • Cardiac magnetic resonance imaging is used to identify ARVD or other primary cardiomyopathies. It is the preferred test to identify these disorders and should be done if other causes of sudden cardiac arrest are not discovered.

  • Signal-averaged ECG: may also be used to identify ARVD if other causes of sudden cardiac arrest are not discovered. Late potentials suggest the diagnosis.

  • Electrophysiological studies: evaluation of primary arrhythmia or conduction abnormalities should be considered if no other cause of sudden cardiac arrest is found, or if there is potential for ablation of an arrhythmogenic source in patients with prior MI. Electrophysiological studies will delineate the arrhythmogenic focus. [36] [41] [42]

  • Screening of family members of patients with suspected or confirmed heritable syndromes.

Risk factorsshow all

Strong

coronary artery disease
  • The most common cause of sudden cardiac arrest is CAD. [2] Transitively, the risk factors for MI and ischaemic heart disease also increase the risk of sudden cardiac arrest. However, given that many people with CAD do not have cardiac arrest, the degree of association of each of these risk factors with sudden cardiac arrest is unsubstantiated. [23]
left ventricular dysfunction
  • Strongly associated with sudden cardiac arrest, whether it is the result of ischaemic heart disease or non-ischaemic dilated cardiomyopathy. [24] This is borne out by studies that show that the implantation of a defibrillator without a history of sudden cardiac arrest markedly reduces the rate of death due to ventricular arrhythmia in this group of patients. [25]
hypertrophic cardiomyopathy (HCM)
  • Disordered myofibrils, myocardial scarring, and ischaemia all likely serve as an arrhythmic substrate in patients with HCM. [26] Though the overall risk of sudden cardiac arrest in these patients is low, HCM is the number one cardiovascular cause of sudden cardiac arrest in young people. [27]
arrhythmogenic right ventricular dysplasia (ARVD)
  • Genetic disorder in which the right ventricular myocardium (and sometimes the left ventricular myocardium) is replaced by a fibro-fatty infiltrate. Retrospective case series in patients with ARVD estimate that almost one quarter of patients have sudden cardiac death as their presenting feature, usually before the fourth decade of life. [28]
long QT syndrome (LQTS)
  • Due to either the inheritance of dysfunctional cardiac ion channels, or an acquired disorder due to various different medicines. [20] Untreated, patients with the congenital LQTS are reported to have a risk of sudden cardiac arrest up to 60% at 10 years. [29] The risk is markedly decreased in patients on beta-blocker treatment, though high-risk features include a QT interval >500 milliseconds, certain mutations, female sex, and history of syncope. [30]
acute medical or surgical emergency
  • Potential causes of cardiac arrest include pulmonary embolism, hypoxia, hypovolaemia, hyper- or hypokalaemia, hydrogen ion excess (acidosis), hypothermia, hypo- or hyperglycaemia, trauma, tension pneumothorax, thrombus (PE, MI), toxins (e.g., in renal failure or drug intoxication), cardiac tamponade, and intracranial pathology.

Weak

Brugada syndrome
  • Characterised electrocardiographically by a pseudo-right bundle branch block pattern and ST-segment elevations in leads V1 through V3. [31] The disorder is thought to be due to the autosomal dominant SCN5A gene mutation, which encodes a dysfunctional cardiac sodium channel and predisposes to arrhythmia, though the known mutations in this gene are found in only 18% to 30% of patients with the syndrome. [32]
  • Notably, the syndrome is estimated to cause up to 4% of sudden cardiac arrest overall, and up to 20% of sudden cardiac arrest in patients without structural heart disease. [32]
smoking
  • Cigarette smoking is associated with an increased risk of sudden cardiac arrest. [33] This may be due to the nicotine-mediated effects of increased myocardial oxygen demand, induction of catecholamine release, coronary vasospasm, and related arrhythmogenicity. [34]
