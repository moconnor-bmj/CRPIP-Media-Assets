Treatment options

BMJ Best Practice is intended for use by licensed medical health professionals and it lists drug names by recommended International Nonproprietary Names (rINNs) only. It is possible that certain drug formularies might refer to the same drugs using US Adopted Names (USANs). Please note that formulations and doses between these two drug nomenclatures may differ. Consequently, the information provided must be checked in each instance in order to ensure that it is accurate and suitable. Professional judgement must be exercised in order to provide appropriate treatment. In particular, consult your local pharmaceutical resource for drug information, including with regard to contraindications, drug interactions, and alternative dosing. (see Disclaimer)

Patient group:

unwitnessed cardiac arrest

1st

 

1stCPR

  • Circulation of the blood by means of CPR is a demonstrated therapy in patients with sudden cardiac arrest and is thought to work by raising intrathoracic pressure, as well as providing direct cardiac compression. [69]

  • Each cycle: 30 compressions (at a rate of 100 compressions/minute) and 2 breaths for a total of 5 cycles (2 minutes). [48]

  • International Liaison Committee on Resuscitation (ILCOR) guidelines have specified that compressions should be performed as the first priority; breaths may follow, but should not impede lay persons from providing emergent compressions, as compression-only CPR has yielded equivalent outcomes to compression plus ventilation. [48]

  • The patient should be transferred to an emergency care setting as soon as possible.

Patient group:

shockable rhythms (pulseless ventricular tachycardia or ventricular fibrillation)

1st

 

1stCPR and defibrillation

  • Circulation of the blood by means of CPR is a demonstrated therapy in patients with sudden cardiac arrest and is thought to work by raising intrathoracic pressure, as well as providing direct cardiac compression. [69]

  • Each cycle: 30 compressions (at a rate of 100 compressions/minute) and 2 breaths for a total of 5 cycles (2 minutes). [48]

  • International Liaison Committee on Resuscitation (ILCOR) guidelines have specified that compressions should be performed as the first priority; breaths may follow, but should not impede lay persons from providing emergent compressions, as compression-only CPR has yielded equivalent outcomes to compression plus ventilation. [48]

  • Defibrillation in the setting of pulseless VT/VF is demonstrated to restore normal sinus rhythm and should be initiated promptly, as the success of this therapy diminishes over time. [70]

  • For a witnessed arrest, electrical defibrillation should be attempted as soon as possible, not necessarily after 5 cycles of CPR. [56]

  • For unwitnessed out-of-hospital cardiac arrest, 5 cycles of CPR before defibrillation yields more favourable outcomes. [71]

  • Guidelines recommend 1 shock followed by CPR if a perfusing rhythm is not restored, because the benefit of multiple shocks is undemonstrated and the delay in resumption of CPR is harmful. [56]

  • Guideline recommendations are 120 to 200 J for biphasic defibrillators and 360 J for monophasic defibrillators. [49]

plus

vasopressor

  • Given with second shock and followed with 5 cycles of CPR.

  • Adrenaline (epinephrine) is a potent agonist of alpha- and beta-adrenergic receptors. Despite controversial data on its benefit in resuscitation, it has been used extensively in treating patients of sudden cardiac arrest and occupies a prominent role in the algorithms of advanced cardiac life support. [49]

  • In cases where intravenous access cannot be established, twice the normal dose of adrenaline (epinephrine) (diluted in saline) can be given through an endotracheal tube. [1]

  • Vasopressin is normally secreted by the posterior pituitary gland. In addition to its myriad effects on the kidney, vasopressin is also a potent vasoconstrictor. It should not be used instead of adrenaline (epinephrine), but can continue to be used in settings where it is already in use. [49]

Primary options

adrenaline: 1 mg intravenously every 3-5 minutes

anti-arrhythmic

  • Given after adequate CPR and 2 or 3 attempts at defibrillation.

  • Amiodarone is predominantly a class III anti-arrhythmic agent that also has alpha- and beta-adrenergic blocking properties. Trials on use in the setting of VT/VF have shown an increased rate of survival to hospital admission in comparison with placebo [72] or lidocaine [73] in out-of-hospital sudden cardiac arrest, but ultimately no significant difference in the rate of hospital discharge. [B Evidence]

  • Use of lidocaine in VT/VF is predominantly due to studies of efficacy in suppressing ventricular arrhythmias following acute MI. Studies have shown that it is inferior to amiodarone with respect to rates of survival to hospital admission in patients of out-of-hospital sudden cardiac arrest. [73] [A Evidence] Its use is supported as an alternative to amiodarone.

Primary options

amiodarone: 300 mg intravenously as a single dose initially, followed by 150 mg as a single dose if required

atropine

  • Given during CPR if bradycardic PEA and asystole are present.

Ongoing

Patient group

Treatment line

Treatmentshow all

Patient group:

return of spontaneous circulation

1st

 

1stpost-resuscitation care

  • Should be instigated immediately and involves continued monitoring, organ support, correction of electrolyte imbalances and acidosis, safe transfer to a critical care environment, and identification and correction of risk factors and underlying causes.

  • Multiple studies of therapeutic hypothermia (lowering the core body temperature to 32 to 34°C (89.6 to 93.2°F) have demonstrated an improvement in neurological function and survival without a significant increase in the rate of adverse events. [76]

  • Long-term management focuses primarily on prevention of recurrence. Patients should abstain from toxic substances. Comparison of antiarrhythmic therapy with the use of implantable cardioverter-defibrillators (ICD) has shown a significant reduction in mortality with the use of ICDs compared with drug therapy in the secondary prevention of sudden cardiac arrest. [67]

no return of spontaneous circulation

1st

 

1stcontinue or consider termination of resuscitation

  • The decision to terminate resuscitation is an ethically challenging issue when treating patients for whom circulation does not return in a timely fashion.

  • Terminating resuscitative measures may be legitimate on the basis of the following parameters: delayed initiation of CPR in unwitnessed cardiac arrest, unsuccessful resuscitation after 20 minutes of ACLS guideline-directed therapy, documentation that a patient has previously signed a "do not resuscitate" order or conditions that compromise the safety of the emergency care providers.
